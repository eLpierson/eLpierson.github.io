What We Do

You’re a residential real estate agent. Your clients trust you on the inspection report. Now you can too. InspectConnect reads your client’s inspection report, surfaces what actually matters, and tells you how to advise so you walk into every post-inspection conversation prepared. Stop spending hours parsing 80-page PDFs. Start closing deals with confidence.

Why This Exists

Real Estate Agents

Every residential sale ends with an 80+ page inspection report that agents are expected to interpret and advise on — even though it’s not their job. They’re not contractors, engineers, or inspectors. The industry treats this as table stakes: a good agent knows the report and guides their client through it. The insight is that the report genuinely does require judgment — it’s often the reason a deal falls apart — but agents are exercising that judgment without the preparation to do it well. After every inspection, they spend 3–5 hours going back and forth with their client: figuring out what actually matters, prioritizing issues, recommending contractors, getting quotes, estimating costs, and building a negotiation strategy — for a document they were never trained to interpret, in a role they were never licensed to fill. InspectConnect gives them the preparation so the judgment they bring is actually grounded.

Homebuyers

9 out of 10 homebuyers rely on their agent to tell them what the inspection report means and what to do about it. The accepted belief is that the agent knows — they’ve seen enough deals to understand what’s serious and what isn’t. The insight is two-layered. First: most agents don’t actually know what they’re talking about when it comes to the technical findings. They aren’t contractors or inspectors, and the report wasn’t written for them. Second: the agents who do understand it are often not legally permitted to advise on it — recommending repairs, assessing severity, or directing contractors creates liability exposure they’re not licensed to carry. But they advise anyway, because their client has nowhere else to turn. The buyer is either getting guidance from someone who doesn’t know, or from someone who knows but legally shouldn’t be saying it. InspectConnect closes that gap without putting the agent at risk.

Contractors

Post-inspection leads close faster and at higher conversion rates than any cold marketplace lead — the problem is documented, the scope is defined, and a trusted advisor is already in the room. The industry believes the best place to find home service demand is a marketplace where homeowners search. The insight is that the highest-intent moment isn’t when someone searches Angi. It’s the 48 hours after an inspection report lands. That window has never had a product built for it. InspectConnect puts the right contractor in front of the right buyer at exactly that moment — before they ever open a browser.

What we learned

The broker angle is liability. Brokers care about liability shield — not just differentiation or agent productivity. When an agent gives advice on an inspection report without expertise, the brokerage carries the risk. InspectConnect gives agents something defensible to point to. This reframes our value prop for brokerage-level sales entirely.

What we got wrong

Cost estimates upfront may be counterproductive. We assumed showing cost estimates immediately was a core value driver. Early feedback suggests it may actually scare off both buyers and agents — sticker shock before context. We are rethinking where cost estimates belong in the workflow. They may be more powerful as a second-step reveal after the buyer has reviewed the issues, not as a lead feature.

What we’re still uncertain about

Whether Henryk’s pilot converts to full brokerage adoption. Whether liability framing changes our go-to-market messaging. Where cost estimates should live in the workflow — upfront, on-demand, or after the buyer signals interest in a specific issue.

Same pain, different product shape.

How You Can Help

Two beachheads, same pain, different product shape — parallel or sequential?

SF and Chicago surfaced organically through contact density — we didn’t choose them. The core problem is identical in both markets: agents navigating complex documents they weren’t trained to interpret. But the shape of the product is different. Chicago is a single inspection report processed into negotiation guidance. SF is that same report sitting inside a 10–20 document disclosure pack that agents need to run all at once. It’s not two different products — it’s the same product with meaningfully different scope and workflow. Our question is whether absorbing that nuance across two markets simultaneously sharpens us or spreads us thin. Where have you seen early-stage teams navigate this well?

Data asset to data moat

We are building a contractor dataset that will be 9x Angi’s total database (2.3M vs 250K). Data accumulation is an asset; a moat requires it to compound into something competitors can’t replicate.
