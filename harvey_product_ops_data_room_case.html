Context & Assumptions

Market position

Harvey's Data Room enters a market that is only starting to evolve after 20-something years. The biggest competitors in this space are adding AI-driven features, but are not AI companies, whereas Harvey is. Some competitors are boasting long feature lists as a competitive advantage (Datasite, for example). Our value prop is that Harvey is the only purpose-built environment where document storage and AI interrogation are native to each other (not bolted together) with answers traceable back to a source.

The trust bar

We know that law firms don't tend to be early adopters, and they don't quickly forget if a tool has failed them, so the bar here is enormously high. They operate under strict confidentiality obligations and are personally liable for the advice that they give. Trust needs to be a first principle in this launch.

Product Ops posture

At a company Harvey's size, a Product Ops function can either clear the path or become overhead. The version that works owns the feedback system, the phase gate process, and cross-functional reporting, but stays out of roadmap decisions, feature prioritization, and anything Legal has already decided. The goal is to make the PM's job easier, not to add a layer between them and the product.

Working Assumptions

A1 The engineering team is small and moving fast. Work that moves forward here is that which adds signal, not process.
A2 Harvey's customers have strict data privacy obligations that are a condition of doing business rather than a box to check: attorney-client privilege, cross-border deal confidentiality, GDPR, SEC and FCA document handling, and equivalent frameworks in APAC and other jurisdictions where Harvey operates.
A3 Harvey is expanding internationally at speed: 700 customers across 58 countries as of late 2025, with a €50M raise specifically to accelerate that. The Data Room product should be built for a global rollout from the start, not retrofitted for it later. Alpha and beta cohorts should include international firms by design, not as an afterthought.
A4 The M0–M3 milestone sequence is fixed by ProdEng. This document is the operating layer on top of that sequence.

Alpha

Phase 1: Alpha Weeks 1–6

Alpha is not a soft launch. It's a structured way to find what's broken before it breaks in a real deal.

Who to Bring In

Three to five existing Harvey customers who meet all of these criteria:

Active deals generating real due diligence documents in that window. No sample data.
A track record of giving honest, specific feedback, not just positive responses because they like Harvey.
Legal team comfortable signing a participation agreement that is clear about the pre-release status of the software.
At least one large firm, one mid-market firm, and one in-house legal team. Different environments surface different problems.

When beta starts, we carry forward three of the alpha firms rather than all five. Three is the right number because it keeps the beta group manageable while M1 stabilizes, and it preserves slots for fresh perspectives from firms that have not yet seen the product.

The three we graduate are chosen on these criteria, in this order:

1.Actual usage depth during alpha: which firms put real deal documents in and ran the product hard, not just in demos. Usage logs tell us this.
2.Quality of feedback: which firms surfaced specific, reproducible issues rather than general impressions. The firms that found edge cases in alpha will find them in beta too, and that is what we need.
3.Geographic and firm-type fit: we want at least one international firm in the beta group from week one, not week nine. If an alpha firm covers that slot, they graduate ahead of a domestic firm with equivalent usage and feedback scores.

How We Run It

Kick-off call (45 min): what the product can and can't do, how to flag issues, what the timeline is. Be upfront there will be problems, and surfacing bugs is part of the point. Response times made explicit: anything that could expose data or produce an incorrect answer gets addressed within 2 hours. Everything else within 24 hours.
Dedicated Slack channel per firm with direct PM and engineer access (not routed through the CSM). When something breaks mid-deal, the customer needs to reach someone who can fix it, not open a ticket. The CSM stays in the loop but isn't the middle-person.
Weekly check-ins (CSM-led, 20 min). Structured: what worked, what didn't, what's confusing, what's missing.
Slack feedback template after each session. Alpha volume is low enough that this is sufficient. The automated feedback system gets built for beta when volume makes manual collection impractical.

Aim for 90% Accuracy

Accuracy in legal due diligence is more demanding than in most products:

A response is only accurate if the cited source directly supports the answer. Partial support is a failure.
A technically correct answer that omits a material risk is also a failure. Attorneys make decisions on completeness, not just correctness.
Knowing when not to answer confidently is a feature. An AI that says "I'm not certain" when it isn't is more useful than one that sounds definitive when it shouldn't.

The benchmark: ~100 questions drawn from real M&A due diligence types. For example, we build the Q&A set from acquisition agreement terms, IP representations, employment agreements, and environmental disclosures before the first alpha customer onboards. A practicing M&A attorney reviews the answers, not the product team. The 90% target is against their standard, not ours. We make sure they are aligned with 90% for alpha knowing we will increase from here, its just a starting point.

Alpha Exit Criteria — all must be true, not most
No open bugs that could expose data or produce a legally wrong answer
Four of five alpha firms say they'd use the product in a live deal today
AI accuracy at or above 90% on the attorney-reviewed benchmark (see accuracy notes above)
Hallucination rate (where the cited source doesn't support the answer) below 2%
Legal has signed off on how we're handling data
Beta

Phase 2: Beta Weeks 7–16

Beta adds more customers and introduces M1. Here we prove that the product works at scale and that the feedback system can run without someone pulling it together manually every week.

Expansion

Weeks 7–8: Three alpha graduates plus five new CSM-nominated customers. Keep it small while M1 stabilizes.
Weeks 9–12: Expand to 15–25 total. Prioritize a mix of firm sizes, deal types, and geographies. We are targeting at least 30% international representation, and not just European firms for GDPR coverage, but customers in APAC and other regions where Harvey is actively growing. Different jurisdictions surface different document types, languages, and regulatory frameworks that the product needs to handle before GA.
Weeks 13–16: Measurement window. Cohort is fully onboarded, feedback is running, and we're evaluating against exit criteria before voting 'Go' on GA date.

Feedback: Automated Ingestion, Categorization, and Routing

Alpha ran on a simple Slack template since volume didn't require more. At 25 customers across multiple time zones, manual collection breaks down. Beta is where we build the automated feedback system that will run through GA and beyond.

01
Ingestion
Every feedback signal enters through one of three channels: the in-product thumbs up/down prompt shown after every AI response (with an optional free-text field and a dropdown to tag issue type), the structured Slack template used in weekly check-ins, and direct bug reports routed through a Jira intake form linked from the Slack channel. All three feed into a single Airtable base that is the system of record.
02
Automated Categorization
When a submission arrives, a lightweight classifier (built on the same AI infrastructure Harvey already runs) that tags each item on two dimensions automatically. The first dimension is issue type: AI accuracy failure, UX friction, missing feature, compliance concern, or performance issue. The second dimension is severity: blocking, degrading, or minor. High-confidence tags are applied automatically. Low-confidence submissions get flagged for manual review by Product Ops within 24 hours.
03
Routing and Dissemination
Once tagged, items route automatically. AI accuracy failures go to a shared PM and engineering Slack channel with the original submission, the auto-generated test case, and a link to the Airtable record. Compliance concerns go to a Legal and PM thread with a 24-hour acknowledgment requirement. Performance issues go to the engineering on-call rotation. Feature requests and UX issues go to the PM backlog queue, deduplicated by similarity so the PM sees "seven customers asked for this" rather than seven separate items.
Beta Exit Criteria — all must be true, not most
AI accuracy at or above 95% on the attorney-reviewed benchmark
Hallucination rate below 1%
NPS above the alpha baseline from the beta cohort
No more than one critical bug per week across the cohort in the final two weeks
Infrastructure load test passed and uptime validated at 99.9%, which is the test that earns the GA readiness claim. The test runs in weeks 13–14 against realistic load (50 simultaneous data rooms with 10,000+ documents each), across multiple regions. If this number needs to be calibrated against actual usage projections from the beta cohort, that should happen in week 12.
Pricing agreed on by Finance and Sales
SOC 2 Type II audit underway with no findings that would block launch
Support runbook exists and CS team is trained on it
GA

Phase 3: General Availability Weeks 17–20

GA is a coordinated release, not the flip of a switch. Weekly readiness calls start in week 15. This means we name one dependency: week 15 is the second-to-last week of beta, so if beta needs to extend, the launch date moves with it.

GTM and Marketing

Marketing is not a week-20 event. It is a thread that runs through all four phases and should be treated as part of the operational plan, not a downstream output of it.

During alpha, the marketing team's job is listening, not publishing. Alpha participants should be asked explicitly for stories, like specific moments where the product saved time or caught something a paralegal would have missed. These become the raw material for launch content, and they need to come from real deals. The PM and marketing lead should align in week one on what a quotable outcome looks like and how to capture it without violating confidentiality.

During beta, marketing shifts to building the launch narrative. The core message, that Harvey is the only purpose-built environment where document storage and AI interrogation are native to each other and not bolted together, should be tested with beta customers before it is published. Beta is also where we identify the partner-level attorney who will co-author the launch post. This person needs time to review, contribute, and approve, a process that typically takes three to four weeks, which means outreach starts no later than week 10.

At GA, the launch itself follows a relationship-first sequence. Beta participants and top existing customers hear about it directly from their CSM before anything is published. The week-19 in-app notification and email that goes to all existing customers is not a cold outreach; it's a follow-up to a relationship that already exists. New inbound from the public launch in week 20 is handled by Sales, not marketing automation.

Rollout

Week 17: Beta participants get access automatically.
Week 18: CSMs reach out personally to the top 50 existing Harvey customers by contract value. A real conversation or a personalized demo invitation, not a mass email. This is consistent with how Harvey sells: relationship-first, not inbound-first.
Week 19: Data Room goes live in the Harvey dashboard for all existing customers. An in-app notification goes out. Sales refresh call.
Week 20: Public launch. Blog post, press, social. Inbound opens.

How We Measure Success

During Alpha and Beta — Is the Product Working?
AI accuracy: attorney-reviewed against the 100-question benchmark. Target is 90% at end of alpha, 95% at end of beta. Below threshold means we hold the phase.
Hallucination rate: % of AI responses where the cited source doesn't support the answer. Target below 2% in alpha, below 1% in beta.
Field extraction accuracy: % of fields the AI pulls correctly vs. a paralegal ground truth on a 50-document test set. Target 92% or better. Measured in beta when M1 is live.
Document processing: % of uploaded files that process successfully. Target above 99%. Tracked by file type because failures won't be random.
Bug count per week by severity. In beta, more than two critical or high bugs in a single week means the sprint reprioritizes to quality.
After GA — Is It Driving Value?
Time from data room creation to first AI question. Target under 30 minutes. This is the clearest signal the product delivers value without hand-holding.
Weekly active data rooms, meaning rooms with actual AI activity, not just created. Separates real adoption from shelfware.
NPS at 30 and 60 days post-activation. Target above the beta cohort baseline. The 90-day read is reviewed in the third post-GA sprint, and it's a lagging signal that can't be evaluated during the four-week GA phase.
How many customers explicitly migrated from Datasite or Intralinks. Tracked in CRM by Sales. The most direct proof point for the category positioning.

Go / No-Go Framework

Hold if any of these are true
An open bug that could expose customer data or produce a wrong answer someone might act on
AI accuracy below threshold and we don't know why; a known, fixable issue is different from an unexplained regression
Legal or Security has flagged a blocker, not just a concern
In alpha specifically: fewer than 4 of 5 firms say they'd use the product in a live deal, and the reason is the product rather than hesitation about change
Move forward when
All exit criteria are met
Open non-blocking items each have a clear owner and a realistic timeline
If I'm the one saying we should hold, I come with a specific plan to get unblocked, not just a red status

The most important habit post-GA: when a customer request gets built, their CSM tells them. Most teams track whether a feature shipped. Fewer track whether the customer who asked for it knows. That gap is the difference between customers who feel like partners and customers who feel like users.
