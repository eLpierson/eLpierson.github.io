Key Learnings & Pivots

1. Inspector rev share is prohibited — and that's a good thing

What looked like a setback (losing 20–30% of lead revenue to inspectors) turned into an improvement. Without inspector variable costs, our contribution margin jumps to ~80%. Inspectors still participate because the platform adds value to their report delivery at zero cost. The regulatory constraint simplified our model and eliminated our biggest investor concern.

2. The agent is the retention unit, not the buyer

Homebuyers transact once every 7–10 years. Agents process 20–40 inspections per year. Agent LTV ($2,400–$4,800/year for 3+ years) is a more compelling retention story than buyer LTV ($105–175 one-time). This reframes the entire unit economics narrative around recurring revenue from agent relationships.

3. Demand pull is real — users are describing our product before we describe it

Radha Tomassetti (Compass) said "It would be great if I had a service to help educate homeowners alongside me" — without knowing InspectConnect exists. Harrison Cohen asked to invest during a discovery interview. These are textbook product-market fit signals at the awareness stage.

4. We built faster than we activated

Three weeks of intense product development produced a live, demo-able MVP. But the Antler kickoff's north star was: "Get real leads flowing through agent partners." We have the product and the commitments. The gap is activation. March is about closing that gap.
